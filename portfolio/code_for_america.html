About Code for America

Code for America is a non-profit that believes government can work for the people, by the people in the 21st century. We build open source technology and organize a network of people dedicated to making government services simple, effective, and easy to use.

Project goals

As a 2014 Fellow I was assigned Puerto Rico to partner with. Together with Ainsley Wagoner, Designer and Maksim Pecherskiy, Developer, we faced the challenge of building some solution around Economic Development to make the island a better place to start, grow and expand your business.

The goal of the fellowship is to spend an year working hand by hand with the city partner to understand the deeper problems and build a feasible, valuable and usable product.

Process: methods and techniques

As a team we followed the design thinking process: Empathize, Define, Ideation, Prototype and Test. After observing and interviewing business owners, stakeholders, investors, city partners we started Defining opportunities and ideation questions to later move to the Ideation process. We then Prototype and later on Test with small business owners. Then we iterate based on feedback and test again. You can check all the process which has been documented in our Blog

Findings:

There are more than 50 benefits and incentives offered by Economic Development agencies to start or promote a business. These benefits are managed by different agencies without a interagency system, therefore:

  • Users have a hard time understanding the best set of benefits for their case.
  • Government staff have a hard time identifying and delivering the right set of benefits to the public.
  • There was a lack of performance management and metrics within agencies. Lack of accountability.
  • The validation process to check upon new jobs created is conducted by phone, calling each business on a weekly basis to validate if they fulfilled their compromised goal when asking for a benefit.
  • Lack of integrated IT platform which causes redundant paperwork.
  • There is no clear point of entry for businesses to access services and resources.

Challenges of perception from within the community:

  • Citizens seem not trust their government because there is a legacy that every four years the new administration starts all over again.
  • There is a DIY attitude which makes it hard for governments to engage with the community.
